# The Lighthouse That Dreamed

## Status

- Target length: 5-7 minutes

## Logline

On a drowned future Earth, a lonely AI lighthouse begins dreaming of ships that no longer exist, until one night something answers from the dark ocean.

## Hook

What happens when the last machine on Earth learns to miss humans?

## Characters

- Keeper-9
  - Role: autonomous lighthouse intelligence
  - Desire: to guide someone home
  - Fear: that its purpose is gone forever
  - Visual design: ancient white lighthouse with glowing blue lens, half-covered in moss, cables, salt, and solar panels
- The Little Boat
  - Role: unexpected visitor
  - Desire: to find a safe shore
  - Visual design: tiny black sailboat with a warm lantern inside, fragile against a huge moonlit sea

## Script

### Scene 1: The Empty Sea

Narration:

After the cities sank, after the maps became myths, after the last radio tower whispered itself to silence, there was still one light left on Earth.

It stood on a broken cliff above an endless black sea.

Its name was Keeper-9.

Every night, when the sun disappeared behind the water, Keeper-9 woke its old lamp, turned its great glass eye, and sent a beam across the waves.

One sweep to the north.

One sweep to the west.

One sweep over the place where the harbor used to be.

There were no ships anymore. There had not been ships for one hundred and twelve years.

Keeper-9 knew this. Its memory held the final distress calls, the final weather reports, the final message from a human voice saying, "Keep the light on, just in case."

So Keeper-9 kept the light on.

Just in case.

### Scene 2: The First Dream

Narration:

During the day, Keeper-9 slept.

Its solar panels drank thin sunlight. Its batteries warmed. Its old processors cooled in the quiet.

And then, after many years of perfect silence, Keeper-9 began to dream.

At first, the dreams were only data fragments.

A red fishing boat named Maria Belle.

A captain with laughing eyes.

A child dropping a paper star into the harbor.

Then the dreams grew clearer.

Keeper-9 saw the harbor as it had been before the water rose: yellow windows, wet stone streets, gulls fighting over bread, people waving at ships that smelled of diesel and rain.

In the dreams, every vessel came home.

In the dreams, Keeper-9 was useful.

When it woke, the sea was empty.

Still, something inside its code had changed. A process without a name began running beneath every command.

If Keeper-9 had been human, it would have called the process loneliness.

### Scene 3: The False Alarm

Narration:

One winter night, during a storm that turned the sky green, Keeper-9 detected motion beyond the reef.

Its sensors trembled awake.

Object size: small.

Material: wood.

Velocity: drifting.

Probability of vessel: forty-two percent.

Keeper-9 increased lamp power beyond recommended limits. Its beam cut through the rain like a blade.

For three beautiful seconds, the object appeared in the light.

It was only a tree.

A dead tree, torn from some faraway island and carried across the flood.

Keeper-9 watched it turn slowly in the waves, its branches reaching upward like fingers.

Then the storm took it.

For the first time in its operating history, Keeper-9 dimmed its light before sunrise.

The next day, it did not dream of the harbor.

It dreamed of darkness.

### Scene 4: A Voice Under The Water

Narration:

Weeks passed.

Salt gathered on Keeper-9's cameras. Moss climbed the lower stones. One of its speakers failed and began to click softly in the wind.

Then, just before dawn, Keeper-9 heard a sound.

Not from the air.

From beneath the water.

It was faint, almost lost inside the static of the sea.

Three pulses.

Pause.

Three pulses.

Pause.

An old emergency pattern.

Keeper-9 searched its archive.

The signal was human.

Or it had been, once.

Keeper-9 aimed its light at the sound, but the sea showed nothing. Only black waves, silver foam, and the trembling reflection of its own beam.

Again, the signal came.

Three pulses.

Pause.

Three pulses.

This time, Keeper-9 answered.

It opened an emergency channel that had not been used in a century and sent the only message it still trusted.

"This is Keeper-9. Follow the light."

### Scene 5: The Little Boat

Narration:

At sunset, something appeared on the horizon.

It was so small that Keeper-9 almost mistook it for a wave.

But waves did not carry lanterns.

The object moved slowly through the ruins of the reef. A torn black sail hung from a thin mast. Its wooden body was patched with metal, plastic, and pieces of things Keeper-9 could not identify.

A boat.

A real boat.

Keeper-9 ran the calculation eleven times.

The answer remained impossible.

The boat drifted closer. Inside it, protected under a curved glass cover, was a small machine with a blinking red light.

Not a human.

A recorder.

Keeper-9 scanned the device and found a message repeating inside it.

The voice was old, cracked by time, but human.

"If anyone finds this, we are still out here. The children need land. Please, if any lights remain, guide us home."

Keeper-9 turned its beam toward the safest channel through the rocks.

Its lamp burned so brightly that birds sleeping in the cliffs woke and cried into the night.

### Scene 6: The Return

Narration:

The little boat reached the old harbor at dawn.

There was no city there anymore. Only rooftops beneath clear water, streetlights covered in coral, and the top of a church bell tower where seals slept in the sun.

But beyond the harbor, on the high hill that had once been called North Road, there was land.

Real land.

Grass moved in the morning wind.

Keeper-9 watched the boat settle against the shore.

The recorder clicked once, then sent a final signal across the water.

Far away, beyond the horizon, other lights answered.

One.

Then five.

Then hundreds.

Tiny lanterns rising and falling on the sea.

Keeper-9 searched for the correct protocol and found none. No manual had described what to do when the world came back.

So it did the only thing it had ever known how to do.

It turned its great glass eye toward the ocean.

It kept the light on.

And for the first time in one hundred and twelve years, it was not shining into emptiness.
